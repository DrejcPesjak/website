Scaling Monosemanticity on LLaMA

Notes from replicating “Scaling Monosemanticity” style sparse autoencoders on LLaMA. This is a practical write-up: what worked, what broke, and what I’d do differently next time.

Sparse Autoencoders LLaMA HPC Mechanistic Interpretability

Why I did this

I wanted hands-on experience with SAE training on modern transformer models, and to understand where “nice demo results” survive contact with real infrastructure: multi-GPU training, memory limits, and messy checkpoints.

Setup in one paragraph

The core loop is straightforward: collect activations from a chosen layer, train a sparse autoencoder to reconstruct them, and then inspect the learned features by activating them and looking at the downstream effect. In practice, most time went into stable data collection, batching, and making sure the run is reproducible on an HPC cluster.

What went wrong (and how I debugged it)

  • Data pipeline bottlenecks: activation extraction can become the slowest part. I ended up caching activations and treating extraction like a dataset-building step.
  • Memory surprises: layer choice and sequence length matter more than expected. Small changes silently doubled VRAM use.
  • Training instability: some runs “look fine” early and then collapse. Logging reconstruction loss + sparsity + feature norms helped catch it fast.
  • Feature interpretability isn’t free: you can get a good loss curve and still learn features that are hard to name. Good evaluation needs more than a single dashboard metric.

What actually mattered

  • Good activation sampling: diversity in prompts and stable preprocessing beat fancy tricks.
  • Clear evaluation criteria: decide early what “success” means (loss, sparsity, downstream behavior, feature usefulness).
  • Reproducibility: fixed seeds, saved configs, and deterministic data shards saved days later.

Lessons learned

The biggest takeaway: SAE training is not the hard part — building a clean pipeline around it is. Once extraction + caching + evaluation are stable, iteration becomes fast and you can actually learn something from the features.

Next steps

  • Compare features across layers (how “semantic” features shift with depth).
  • Test targeted interventions (boost/suppress specific features) on controlled tasks.
  • Document a minimal “starter recipe” for SAE training on your own infra.
